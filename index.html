Comprehensive Guide to Regional Land Area Measurements in India

Land measurement across India is structurally fragmented, relying deeply on age-old local units that change coordinates from state to state. While northern states operate on Bigha, Biswa, and Killa, eastern regions evaluate calculations in Kattha, Dhur, and Lessa. Down south, systems transition into Cent, Ankanam, and Ground. Understanding these local land dynamics is critical for real estate development, legal registry auditing, and agricultural financial operations. Every state has a specialized history tied to land records (Bhulekh), meaning a Bigha in Uttar Pradesh is mathematically completely different from a Bigha in Assam or a Vigha in Gujarat. This definitive manual breaks down every regional constant used by surveyors, revenue boards, and financial institutions to secure precision layout estimations.

Northern India Systems: Bigha, Biswa, Killa, and Kanal

In northern territories like Uttar Pradesh, Punjab, Haryana, Rajasthan, and Himachal Pradesh, land is measured using historical systems heavily tied to agricultural plot dimensions. A prominent variable across these spaces is the Pucca Bigha versus the Kaccha Bigha. In parts of western and eastern UP, a Pucca Bigha equates exactly to 27,225 square feet (or 3,025 square yards), whereas a Kaccha Bigha is structurally exactly one-third of that value, measuring 9,075 square feet.

Furthermore, fractions of a Bigha are calculated as Biswa, where 1 Pucca Bigha equals 20 Biswa. Descending further down the modular ladder, 1 Biswa breaks into 20 Biswansi, which contains 20 Kachwansi. Our mathematical matrices inside the UP Land Calculator Hub account for these precise localized differences, giving users real-time metric updates in square meters, hectares, and standard acres.

Moving towards Punjab and Haryana, the primary revenue terminology shifts to Killa and Kanal. Here, 1 Killa represents a standard traditional Acre (43,560 square feet). This Killa contains exactly 8 Kanals, meaning 1 Kanal measures 5,445 square feet. Each Kanal is further divided into 20 Marlas (with 1 Marla matching 272.25 square feet). In hilly areas of Himachal Pradesh and Uttarakhand, small terrace farmlands make heavy use of Bigha and Biswa constants scaled differently, where a mountain Bigha equals roughly 8,712 square feet.

Eastern India Analytics: Kattha, Lessa, Chatak, and Dhur

In eastern zones such as Bihar, West Bengal, Assam, Tripura, and Jharkhand, calculations adopt terms like Kattha, Dhur, and Lessa. In Assam, land measurement parameters are strict and codified under local settlement laws. Here, 1 standard Assam Bigha matches exactly 14,400 square feet. This Bigha is split into 5 Katthas (where 1 Katha equals 2,880 square feet), and 1 Katha comprises 20 Lessas (making 1 Lessa exactly 144 square feet).

If we look into Bihar, the system morphs depending on the length of the measuring rod, locally called the Lagga. Generally, 1 Bihar Bigha equals 20 Katthas, and 1 Kattha holds 20 Dhurs. A standard baseline Kattha in Patna and surrounding administrative zones typically spans 1,361.25 square feet, making the Bihar agricultural Bigha equal to 27,225 square feet. However, in rural setups where the Lagga size shifts from 4.5 cubits to 6 cubits, the resulting square footage shifts drastically.

In West Bengal, urban layouts near Kolkata evaluate real estate value using Chatak and Cottah (Kattha). One standard Cottah equals 720 square feet, and it contains 16 Chataks (where 1 Chatak maps perfectly to 45 square feet). Large agricultural farmlands across Bengal use the standard universal Hectare and Acre alongside the local standard Bigha of 14,400 square feet. Shayan Smart Tools packs standalone computational micro-converters for each of these permutations.

Southern and Western Systems: Ground, Cent, Guntha, and Vigha

Southern Indian states like Tamil Nadu, Karnataka, Andhra Pradesh, Telangana, and Kerala rely heavily on decimal metrics mixed with standard British legacy units. The most common metric across Tamil Nadu and Andhra Pradesh is the Cent, where 1 standard Acre holds precisely 100 Cents, making 1 Cent equal to 435.6 square feet. For residential real estate layouts, Tamil Nadu heavily relies on the Ground system, where 1 Ground equals 2,400 square feet.

In Karnataka, Maharashtra, and parts of Gujarat, the traditional baseline unit for farmland division is the Guntha. One standard Acre contains exactly 40 Gunthas. Therefore, 1 Guntha measures exactly 1,089 square feet (or 121 square yards). In Maharashtra, landowners track agricultural land via the 7/12 Extract (Satbara Utara), where areas are officially recorded in Hectares, Ares, and Gunthas (where 1 Are matches 100 square meters or approximately 1.089 Gunthas).

In Gujarat, the word changes phonetically to Vigha. A standard Vigha in Gujarat is traditionally calculated as 17,424 square feet (equal to 1,936 square yards). If a plot measures exactly 2.5 Vighas, it rounds up to match 1 standard universal Acre. Down in Kerala, small homestead properties use the **Cent** system alongside the historical **Maruam** and **Perch**, ensuring high parsing detail for residential layouts.

Multi-Triangle Calculations for Irregular ("Tedi-Medi") Boundaries

Real-world fields and plots are rarely shaped in perfect rectangles or squares. To calculate irregular, non-symmetrical, or "Tedi-Medi" zameen boundaries accurately, Shayan Smart Tools features specialized Advanced Polygon and Irregular Land Calculators. By dividing uneven layout parameters into clean triangular calculations using Heron's computational formula, our utility platform minimizes layout measurement vulnerabilities for property owners, registries, and real estate advisors.

When dealing with a non-standard quadrilateral or multi-sided plot, standard length times width equations yield severe errors. The professional method requires dividing the poly-structural space into multiple triangles by measuring explicit corner-to-corner cross diagonals. Once the layout is broken down into simple triangular structures, each triangle's area can be parsed cleanly by tracking the semi-perimeter:

Semi-Perimeter (s) = (a + b + c) / 2
Area of Triangle = √[s * (s - a) * (s - b) * (s - c)]

By summing the definitive areas of all internal coordinate triangles, Shayan Smart Tools delivers the exact gross surface footprint of uneven land layouts. This system prevents transactional discrepancies during asset division, property purchasing, and land registry operations across Indian villages and municipal layouts.
